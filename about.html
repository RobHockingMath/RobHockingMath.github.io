Rob Hocking

Mathematical Artist and Holographer

I often find that I have trouble saying what it is exactly that I do.


Although I am trained as a mathematician - I have a PhD in Math from Cambridge - what I do in reality is much more interdiscplinary. In my work life, I design and code computer algorithms for solving a problems that vary from image processing to seismic imaging to methane leak detection. I don't think that there is any neat label for this kind of work - in the period from 2010-2015, I found that I was usually given the work title "research engineer", a term that I think my then boss made up. These days, the buzzword seems to be "data scientist".


For my personal projects I'm mainly interested in mathematics as an art form. My objective is to take abstract ideas and to make them tangible; my three main mediums for doing soft are 3D prints, glass etching, and most recently digital holography. More often then not, I don't know when I start a project what the object I'm trying to visualize is going to look like, and in that sense I view mathematical art less a process of creation and more one of discovery.


Just to drive that last point home, here's an example of one of my pieces - it's a 3D cross-section of 5D Menger sponge. Did I design it to look that way? No. Do I have any idea why it looks the way that it does? Well, I've managed to figure out why it has the rotational symmetries that it does, but that's as far as I've gotten.
